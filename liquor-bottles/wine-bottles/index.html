Standard Bottle Sizes
 

In most countries, a standard wine bottle size is 750 ml. Historically, this amount was supposedly determined by a glassblower’s maximum lungful of air. The volume of wine in one standard bottle is considered to be a standard ration of wine for two people in one sitting. It is the most widely produced bottle size because of demand for both hospitality and retail consumers. Other common bottle sizes are a demi or half bottle (375 ml), and a magnum (1.5 L). Large format bottles larger than 3 liters are very uncommon and are typically only utilized for highly prestigious, age-worthy wines from Bordeaux, Burgundy, and Champagne.

Why Are There Different Sizes?
Half bottles are a fabulous serving size for consumers who are dining and drinking on their own. Some restaurants will purchase half bottles (and even splits) to reduce the amount of wine wasted for guests who are not going to purchase a standard-sized bottle.

Large format bottles (magnums and larger) have a lower ratio of wine exposed to oxygen in the ullage (bottle headspace), leaving the wine less vulnerable to the effects of oxidation as it ages. Not only do these grand bottles age the wine gracefully, but they’re very impressive to open and serve on special occasions!

What Are My Labeling & Decorative Options?

 

 

1. Pressure Sensitive Labels

Pressure-sensitive labels are a type of label that can be applied to a variety of surfaces using adhesive. They are typically made of paper, plastic, or vinyl and can be printed with a variety of designs. Pressure-sensitive labels are a popular choice for product labeling, as they are easy to apply and remove.

2. In-Mold Labeling (IML)

In-mold labeling is a process that involves applying a label to a plastic part while it is still in the mold. The label is then heated and fused to the part, creating a permanent bond. In-mold labeling is a popular choice for products that are exposed to harsh conditions, as it provides a durable and long-lasting label.

3. Heat Transfer Labeling (HTL)

Heat transfer labeling is a process that involves applying a label to a surface using heat. The label is made of a material that melts when heated, and the adhesive on the back of the label melts and bonds to the surface. Heat transfer labeling is a popular choice for products that are used in high-temperature environments, as it provides a durable and long-lasting label.

4. Applied Ceramic Labeling (ACL)

Applied ceramic labeling is a process that involves applying a ceramic label to a surface. The label is made of a ceramic material that is fired at a high temperature, creating a permanent bond. Applied ceramic labeling is a popular choice for products that are used in harsh environments, as it provides a durable and long-lasting label.

Wine Bottles Products FAQ 

Q: Why are wine bottles 750?

A: The size was chosen because it was a convenient size for shipping and storing wine. The 750 ml bottle is also known as a "fifth" because it is equal to one-fifth of a gallon. This is why you may sometimes see wine bottles labeled as "fifths".

Q: What does the code mean on a wine bottle?

A: That's the lot number. It refers to the bottling date. Most wineries display such codes as a way to trace wine back to the batch from which it was bottled. This can be critical in the event that a product recall becomes necessary.

Q: Does the color of a wine bottle mean anything?

A: Traditionally, dark green wine bottles are used for red wines, while amber wine bottles are usually for sweet white wines. Light green wine bottles are typically for dry white wine. Traditionally, Champagne uses dark to medium green bottles, which serves as protection from damaging elements.
